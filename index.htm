Project 1: Real‑Time EDTA Spray Detection in Blood Collection Vials for Nasmed

Progress focused on selection of the infrared (IR) sensor capable of detecting presence of spray within the blood sample collection vial. The TCRT5000 sensor was selected due to larger field of view capability. Additionally, it has an in‑built daylight blocking filter that cuts out ambient light, which delivers a higher signal‑to‑noise ratio (SNR).

For an initial proof‑of‑concept (POC) device, 10x TCRT5000 breakout boards were procured and an intermediate design was conceptualized. The working principle is that droplets at the bottom of the vial scatter more infrared light and redirect it to the detector, thereby increasing the signal as compared to an empty vial. This difference in the light intensity is utilized to ascertain the presence of sprayed droplets, as shown in the graph below.

A local firm was engaged to come up with a 3D printed prototype for the sensor layer to evaluate more realistic spraying scenarios. The sensor layer was also modified to handle the asymmetry introduced by the label on the vial. Using the same external rack design, the sensor layer was integrated into the rack, as shown below. A microcontroller board was used to receive analog light signals and convert to digital for processing. The microcontroller also has WiFi capabilities and a server was set up on the host computer, so that any device in the network could access the GUI.

A GUI was developed to visualize the various states of the detector i.e. NO VIAL, VIAL, VIAL+SPRAY, with colors of gray, yellow and green, respectively.

Demo at Nasmed on 15 Nov, 2025

Based on some in‑house testing, a demo was set up with Nasmed technical lead, Mr. Prashant and Mr. Suril, Director. Vials with and without spray were procured from the factory and tests were done to ascertain the presence of spray. Clear differences were observed, with minimal recalibration. Additionally, the wireless functionality was demonstrated by setting up a local server and then accessing the GUI from any phone on the network via browser.

A sample video is shown that captures the testing process. This video was captured during in‑house testing, where it is challenging to replicate the spray pattern of the factory. But a splatter of drops at the bottom of the vial works equally well as verified during the field test at Nasmed.

Next steps

Based on the fact that the proof of concept was satisfactory, the next steps are the following:

  1. Move to Phase 2 of the proposal, that involves a custom PCB electronics board design for the sensor layer. An electronics designed has been engaged and we will start the design work in the week of 17/11. This phase is expected to complete in 4–5 weeks of time including manufacturing 5 boards. The design process will involve dimensioning, component selection, electronic schematics, layout, generation of manufacturing files, manufacturing, assembly and testing.
  2. Based on the success of the POC, a patent will be filed on this idea. An IP firm has been engaged and the internal drafting process has been initiated.
